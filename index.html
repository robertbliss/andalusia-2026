UK & EU Free Entry
Since Brexit, UK citizens are no longer EU citizens and do not qualify for EU free entry at Spanish museums. Where entry is listed as "free for EU citizens", UK visitors pay the standard non-EU fee (typically €1.50–€1.50). Always carry your passport to show at ticket desks.

History & Culture
Torre de la Calahorra CultureKids
The medieval tower at the southern end of the Roman Bridge houses a brilliant interactive museum covering the three cultures of Córdoba — Islamic, Jewish and Christian. Each visitor gets an audio guide with a children's version. Miniature models and panoramic views from the top make it a hit with all ages.
Hours: Daily 10am–6pm (winter), 10am–8pm (summer)  ·  Price: ~€5 adults, ~€3 children  ·  Time needed: 1–1.5 hrs
📍 Maps🌐 Website
Alcázar Gardens GardensKidsFree
The Alcázar de los Reyes Cristianos interior is currently closed for refurbishment, but the magnificent terraced gardens — with their fountains, fish ponds and colourful flower beds — remain open and free to visit. A great open space for younger children to run around.
Hours: Gardens currently free and open  ·  Time needed: 45 mins  ·  Note: Interior closed for refurbishment (check before visiting)
📍 Maps
Medina Azahara CultureKids
A UNESCO World Heritage site just 8km west of Córdoba — the ruins of the 10th-century Caliphate palace city. An excellent visitor centre with engaging exhibits brings the ruins to life. Kids can roam the archaeological site while imagining life in medieval Moorish Spain. Best with a guide.
Hours: Tue–Sat 9am–6pm (varies seasonally)  ·  Price: ~€1.50 (EU citizens free · UK: €1.50)  ·  Getting there: Bus or taxi, ~20 mins from city centre  ·  Time needed: 2 hrs
📍 Maps🌐 Website
Palacio de Viana CultureGardens
A Renaissance palace with twelve stunning patios and courtyards, each more beautiful than the last. The Patio de los Gatos is particularly popular with children. The gardens are lovely and the whole complex is far less crowded than the Mezquita.
Hours: Tue–Sun 10am–7pm (shorter in winter)  ·  Price: ~€8 adults, ~€4 children  ·  Time needed: 1.5 hrs
📍 Maps🌐 Website
Galleries & Art
Museum of Fine Arts of Córdoba GalleryFree
A beautifully presented collection of Córdoban and Andalusian art from the 14th to 20th centuries, housed in a gorgeous 16th-century hospital building on the Plaza del Potro. The shaded orange-tree courtyard alone is worth the visit. Best seen alongside the Julio Romero de Torres Museum directly next door.
Hours: Tue–Sat 9am–9pm, Sun 9am–3pm | Closed Mon  ·  Price: Free  ·  Time needed: 45 min–1 hr
📍 Maps
Julio Romero de Torres Museum Gallery
Dedicated entirely to Córdoba's most celebrated painter, famous for his sensual and atmospheric portraits of Andalusian women. The works are vivid, symbolic and unlike anything else — a real discovery if you've not encountered him before. Housed next door to the Fine Arts Museum; the two together make for a perfect morning.
Hours: Tue–Fri 8:15am–8:15pm, Sat 9:30am–5:30pm, Sun 8:15am–2:15pm | Closed Mon  ·  Price: Small fee (check on arrival)  ·  Time needed: 45 min
📍 Maps
C3A — Contemporary Art Centre of Andalusia Contemporary ArtFree
A striking modern building by Nieto Sobejano Arquitectos beside the Roman Bridge, dedicated to large-scale contemporary art installations. Completely free, air-conditioned, and almost always uncrowded — reviewers consistently say it's one of Córdoba's most underrated attractions. Very much a hidden gem.

Drinks Guide — Andalusia
Beer (Cerveza) — Order a caña (small draft) rather than a pint. In Andalusia the local brew is Cruzcampo. More rounds = more free tapas in Granada!

White wine — Ask for vino blanco. A chilled glass of local Andalusian white (Verdejo or Chardonnay) works perfectly with seafood tapas.

Gin & tonic — Hugely popular in Andalusia. The local speciality is Puerto de Indias strawberry gin from Seville — pink-coloured, served with strawberries and red fruits. Just ask for "Puerto de Indias gin-tonic".

Sangria — The classic. More of a tourist order than a local one, but absolutely fine to enjoy. Red wine, fruit and a splash of brandy over ice.

For the kids — Zumo de naranja natural (freshly squeezed orange juice) is exceptional in Spain. Granizado de limón is a traditional lemon slush that children love. Mosto is freshly pressed grape juice — lighter and less sweet than the UK version (always confirm sin alcohol).

⭐ Alhambra Briefing
Everything you need to know for the biggest day of the trip

Friday 10 April — The Alhambra

The most visited monument in Spain. A 13th-century Nasrid palace complex, fortress and gardens sitting at 740m above Granada with views of the Sierra Nevada. Allow the full afternoon. Nasrid Palaces timed entry is 17:30 — do not be late.
Order: 2020400LXLYO  ·  Ref: H0LXLYO/1  ·  🛂 Passports required for all 4 of you

Visit Sequence
~13:00
Arrive — Main Entrance (Puerta de la Justicia)

Walk up from Plaza Nueva (~25 min, steep) or take the Alhambra minibus. Show tickets + passports at entrance. Collect a site map.

👧 Tell the kids: "We're going into a real Moorish palace — like something from Aladdin"
13:30–15:30
Alcazaba — The Military Fortress

The oldest part of the Alhambra — towers, battlements and panoramic views over Granada and the Sierra Nevada. Climb the Torre de la Vela for the best view on the site.

👧 Kids love: Climbing the towers, spotting the city below, walking the battlements "like guards"
15:30–16:30
Partal Palace & Promenade of Towers

A quieter, more relaxed section — a beautiful porticoed palace reflected in a long pool, with terraced gardens and walkways along the outer walls. Good for younger children to wander freely.

16:30–17:15

The Parador café is the best option. Sit down, have a drink and a snack, let everyone recharge. The Nasrid Palaces require focus and energy — arriving tired is a waste.

👧 Good moment to brief the kids on what they're about to see inside the palaces
17:30 SHARP
⭐ Nasrid Palaces — TIMED ENTRY

Present tickets and passports. Entry strictly at your allocated time slot — latecomers are turned away with no refund. The Palaces take 60–90 minutes at a comfortable pace.

👧 Tell the kids to look for: The honeycomb ceilings (muqarnas), the Court of Lions fountain, tiles so detailed they look like lace, and Arabic writing carved into every wall
~19:00
Generalife Gardens (if energy allows)

The royal summer gardens — fountains, cypress trees and terraced flowerbeds. Lovely in the early evening light. Not compulsory if the children are flagging — the palaces are the priority.

~20:30
🥂 Farewell Dinner — Bodegas Castañeda

C. Almireceros 1-3, Granada. No booking needed. Walk in, find a spot at the bar or a table. Free tapa with every drink. The perfect way to end the holiday's biggest day.

What to Look For — Nasrid Palaces
🦁
Court of the Lions
The most famous courtyard in the Alhambra — a central fountain held up by 12 marble lions, each representing a different hour of the day. The surrounding arcade is carved into intricate geometric patterns. One of the great wonders of medieval architecture.
🍯
Muqarnas Ceilings
The extraordinary honeycomb-like stalactite ceilings. The Hall of the Two Sisters has over 5,000 individual plaster cells. Ask the children to count how many "hexagons" they can see — they'll lose count immediately.
🌊
Patio de los Arrayanes
The Court of the Myrtles — a long rectangular reflecting pool flanked by myrtle hedges and a perfectly symmetrical arcade. The reflection of the Torre de Comares in the still water is one of the most photographed images in Spain.
✍️
Arabic Calligraphy
Every wall, arch and column is covered in Arabic text. Most of it repeats the phrase "There is no conqueror but God" — the motto of the Nasrid dynasty. See how many times the children can spot it (though they'll need to recognise the Arabic script first!).
🎨
Azulejo Tilework
The lower walls throughout the palaces are lined with interlocking geometric tiles in deep blue, green, white and ochre. No two panels are quite the same. The precision of 14th-century craftsmen without modern tools is extraordinary.
👑
Hall of the Ambassadors
The largest room in the Alhambra — the throne room of the Nasrid kings, where Columbus presented his plan to sail west to Queen Isabella. The ceiling represents the seven heavens of Islamic cosmology. Look up — it's spectacular.
Essential Tips for the Day
🛂
Passports
Every person needs their passport at the entrance gate. No exceptions, even for children. Have all four ready before you join the queue.
⏰
Nasrid timing is absolute
Your 17:30 slot is non-negotiable. Arrive at the Nasrid entrance 5 minutes early. Latecomers are turned away with no refund — the one non-negotiable rule of the day.
🎒
What to bring
Water bottles, snacks, suncream and a layer (740m altitude — noticeably cooler). Comfortable shoes — it's all cobbles and slopes. No large bags allowed into the Nasrid Palaces.
📸
Photography
Allowed throughout including the Nasrid Palaces. No flash, no tripods. The best light for the Court of Lions is late afternoon — perfect for your 17:30 entry. Don't miss the reflection shots in the Patio de los Arrayanes pool.
🌡️
Temperature
The Alhambra sits at 740m — a good 5°C cooler than the city. Bring a light layer even if it feels warm in Granada below. The Nasrid Palaces can feel cool after sunset.
🚌
Getting there
Walk up from Plaza Nueva (~25 min, steep but manageable) or take the Alhambra minibus from Plaza Isabel la Católica. Uber/taxi drops you at the main car park. Bibo Suites is about 30 min walk uphill.
